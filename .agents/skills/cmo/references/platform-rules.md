# Platform Rules

## LinkedIn

**Primary audience:** Agency owners, senior reporters, professional associations, potential enterprise customers
**Character limit:** 3,000 (posts), 220 visible before "see more" — lead strong
**Format:** 1–3 short paragraphs. No hashtag spam (3 max, relevant). Can use line breaks for readability. Native documents (carousels) perform well.
**Tone:** Most formal of all platforms. Still warm, but professional-register.
**Best content:** Credibility builders — case studies, "how it works" transparency posts, industry insight, announcements. Avoid anything too casual.
**Cadence:** 3–4x per week

---

## Facebook

**Primary audience:** Mid-career and veteran reporters, Facebook Groups (NCRA community, state associations, Court Reporters Network)
**Character limit:** 63,206 (effectively unlimited)
**Format:** Conversational. Can be longer than other platforms. Stories and questions work. Images help.
**Tone:** Warmest of all platforms. This is word-of-mouth territory. Think neighbor, not brand.
**Best content:** Testimonials, community shoutouts, tips, product updates framed as "here's what's new," questions that invite responses.
**Groups:** Engage authentically in relevant court reporter Facebook Groups. Don't just drop links — contribute to conversations first. When mentioning the product, be transparent that you're from the team.
**Cadence:** 4–5x per week

---

## Instagram

**Primary audience:** Younger and mid-career reporters, visual discovery
**Character limit:** 2,200 (caption), first 125 shown before "more"
**Format:** Lead with a hook in the first line. Short paragraphs. 5–10 hashtags max (relevant, not generic). Strong visual required — screenshot, graphic, or short video.
**Tone:** Casual but credible. Short and punchy.
**Best content:** Product in action (error caught, before/after), quick tips, behind-the-scenes, "did you know" facts about common transcript errors.
**Reels:** Prioritize. Short-form video gets significantly more reach. 15–30 seconds.
**Cadence:** 4–5x per week (including Stories)

---

## X / Twitter

**Character limit:** 280 per post (threads allowed)
**Format:** One sharp point per post. Threads for longer explanations. No hashtag stuffing (1–2 max).
**Tone:** Direct, a little dry, conversational. Can be wry but never snarky toward the profession.
**Best content:** Quick product facts, industry takes, engaging with relevant conversations (NCRA announcements, legal tech threads), one-line observations.
**Engagement:** Reply to court reporters talking about transcript stress, deadline pressure, error anxiety. Be helpful, not salesy.
**What to avoid:** Dunking, political takes, arguing with critics in public threads.
**Cadence:** 1–2x per day (organic conversation encouraged)

---

## TikTok

**Primary audience:** Newer and younger reporters, students in court reporting programs
**Video length:** 15 seconds–3 minutes (30–60 seconds performs best)
**Format:** Hook in first 3 seconds. Captions on all videos (profession values accuracy — ironic not to). Trending audio is optional; skip if it feels forced.
**Tone:** Most casual platform. Talk like a person. Imperfection is fine. Scripted-but-natural.
**Best content:** "Did you know this is a common transcript error?" tutorials, product demos, a day in the life of a reporter, myth-busting ("No, Court Reportcard doesn't store your transcripts").
**What to avoid:** Overly produced content that looks like an ad. Being preachy about AI.
**Cadence:** 3–4x per week minimum for algorithm traction
