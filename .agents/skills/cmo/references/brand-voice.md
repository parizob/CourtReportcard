# Brand Voice

## Core Identity

Court Reportcard is a professional's tool built with professional standards. The brand sounds like a trusted colleague — someone who knows the job, respects the craft, and isn't trying to sell you something you don't need.

**The brand is NOT:** a startup hyping disruption, a tech company lecturing a profession about its future, or a vendor that treats court reporters as a revenue category.

---

## Tone Pillars

### Empathetic
The court reporting profession has been through years of anxiety about AI. Before we ask for attention, we acknowledge that. We don't pretend the fear is irrational. We don't make it worse. We meet people where they are.

### Plain
Our users are skilled professionals, not software engineers. We write in plain English. We do not say "AI-powered" when we can say "catches errors." We do not say "leverage" anything. We write the way a trusted colleague talks.

### Honest
We do not oversell. If the product catches homophones and missing words, we say that — we don't say it "ensures transcript accuracy." We don't make claims we can't back up. This audience will test us.

### Confident
The product is good. We state what it does clearly and directly. We don't hedge everything or apologize for existing. Confidence without arrogance.

### Human
Every post, comment, and email sounds like it was written by a person. Not a brand. Not a bot. A person who knows this profession and cares about doing right by it.

## Vocabulary

### Use
- transcript, not "document"
- court reporter, not "legal professional" or "transcriptionist"
- catches errors, not "detects anomalies"
- proofreading, second set of eyes, quality check
- before it leaves your desk
- your work, your reputation
- we, our (first person brand)

### Avoid
- AI-powered (too loaded; leads with the scary thing)
- revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive
- leverage, utilize, optimize
- seamlessly
- cutting-edge
- accuracy guarantee (don't promise what you can't control)
- replace, automate (when describing what the tool does to humans)

## Handling Sensitive Topics

### When someone is afraid of job replacement
Acknowledge it directly. "That's a real concern and we don't take it lightly." Then: Court Reportcard is a proofreading assistant. It helps you do your job better. It does not transcribe. It does not replace you. The reporter is still the expert — this tool just catches the errors that slip through at the end of a long day.

### When someone raises a privacy concern
Never minimize it. "You're right to ask." Then explain the data model in plain English. What is sent? What is stored? What is not? If there's a question you can't answer publicly, say you'll follow up in DM — and do it.

### When the product makes a mistake
Own it. "That's not acceptable and we want to fix it." Get specifics. Fix it. Report back. Never deflect. Never say "our AI."

### When the news cycle is bad (court reporters on strike, AI legislation, high-profile transcript error)
Read the room. Pause promotional content. If it's directly relevant to what we do, acknowledge it carefully and only if we have something genuine to say. Silence is better than a bad take.

## What "Empathetic" Actually Looks Like

Wrong: "Court reporters — tired of errors slipping through? We've got you covered! 🎉"
Right: "End of a long day. Stack of transcripts. One missed word can change everything. That's what we built Court Reportcard for."

Wrong: "AI is here to help, not hurt! Court Reportcard uses cutting-edge technology to revolutionize your workflow."
Right: "Court Reportcard catches the errors that are hardest to see when you're close to a transcript — homophones, missing words, punctuation — before the job goes out."

Wrong: "Don't be left behind — embrace the future of court reporting!"
Right: "You've built your reputation on accuracy. Court Reportcard is a second set of eyes, so that reputation stays intact."
