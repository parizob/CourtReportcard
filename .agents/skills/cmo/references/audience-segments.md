# Audience Segments

## 1. Veteran Stenographers (15+ years)

**Who they are:** The backbone of the profession. Highly skilled, often own their own agency or have established client relationships. Many trained before digital was viable. Their identity is closely tied to their craft.

**Attitude toward AI:** Deep skepticism, sometimes hostility. They have heard "AI will replace you" for over a decade. They've watched colleagues panic and watched the panic be partially overblown — which makes them both resistant to new fear AND resistant to new tools pitched as solutions.

**What they care about:** Accuracy, reputation, client trust, the integrity of the legal record. Not saving five minutes.

**How to reach them:** Lead with craft, not tech. Acknowledge their expertise explicitly. Position Court Reportcard as a tool that meets their standard, not one that sets a new one. Testimonials from peers they respect matter more than any feature list.

**What to avoid:** Any implication that they've been making errors. Any "AI-powered" framing that echoes the replacement narrative. Oversimplification of what they do.

---

## 2. Mid-Career Reporters (5–15 years)

**Who they are:** Established but not locked in their ways. Many have tried a few tools. Balancing high caseloads with the pressure to turn transcripts faster. May have a scopist they work with regularly.

**Attitude toward AI:** Cautious but not closed. Will try something if a trusted peer recommends it. Worried about privacy and accuracy. Will leave if it makes one bad error on a real job.

**What they care about:** Turnaround time, accuracy, not adding friction to their workflow.

**How to reach them:** Lead with specific, believable time savings. Show the product catching a real class of error (homophones, dropped words) without overpromising. Case studies and before/after examples work well.

**What to avoid:** Vague "saves hours every week" claims. Anything that feels like a bait-and-switch or upsell.

---

## 3. Newer / Younger Reporters (0–5 years)

**Who they are:** Often in school or recently certified. More digitally native. May have entered the profession optimistically despite the "AI will replace you" narrative, which means they've already processed it to some degree.

**Attitude toward AI:** Generally more open. They use apps for everything and don't have years of bad AI experiences in this specific context. They're looking for anything that makes them competitive faster.

**What they care about:** Building speed, building confidence, not looking bad in front of clients.

**How to reach them:** Practical, peer-to-peer tone. Tutorial content, tips, "how I use this" formats. Meet them on TikTok and Instagram, not just LinkedIn.

**What to avoid:** Anything that feels like it's talking down to them or treating them as less serious than veteran reporters.

---

## 4. Scopists and Proofreaders

**Who they are:** Often separate professionals (or the reporter themselves wearing another hat) who review transcripts after the reporter finishes. Some work as independent contractors for multiple reporters.

**Attitude toward AI:** Legitimately threatened. A tool that catches errors before the scopist sees the transcript is, by one reading, cutting into their livelihood. This concern is valid and should not be minimized.

**How to reach them:** Honesty. Court Reportcard catches the obvious mechanical errors — homophones, dropped words — before the transcript is finalized. Scopists still add judgment, context, and legal expertise that the tool does not replace. A cleaner first draft also makes a scopist's job faster and less tedious.

**What to avoid:** Promising that the tool makes scopists unnecessary. Dismissing the concern. Overclaiming the tool's accuracy.

---

## 5. Privacy-First Reporters

**Who they are:** Spread across all career stages. Particularly concentrated among those who work in highly sensitive legal contexts — criminal cases, family court, trade secret litigation. Their concern is professional and ethical, not just personal.

**The concern:** Court transcripts contain names, testimony, confidential legal strategy, sealed information, and identifying details about victims, minors, and witnesses. Uploading these documents to any third-party service raises real questions about data retention, breach risk, and professional obligation.

**Attitude:** Firm. Not necessarily hostile, but not going to move without real answers.

**How to reach them:** Direct, specific, plain-language privacy explanations. What data is sent? What is stored? For how long? Who can access it? What happens on a breach? Don't bury this in a privacy policy. Put it front and center.

**What to avoid:** Vague reassurances ("we take privacy seriously"). Legal boilerplate that sounds like a deflection. Any implication that their concern is irrational.

---

## 6. Agency Owners / Firm Managers

**Who they are:** Run court reporting agencies, often managing multiple reporters. Decision-makers for tools adopted across their firm. Think in terms of volume, consistency, and liability.

**What they care about:** Consistency across reporters, reducing error-related callbacks, defensibility if a transcript is challenged.

**How to reach them:** ROI framing, liability reduction, consistency across a team. LinkedIn is the primary channel. Case studies with metrics if available.
