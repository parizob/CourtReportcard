# Post Templates

Templates are starting points. Adapt for the platform (see platform-rules.md) and the current context. Always sound like a person, not a template.

---

## 1. Error Catch (Product Proof)

**Purpose:** Show the product working on a real class of error. Build credibility.

**Structure:**
- Name the error type (homophone, missing word, punctuation)
- Brief note on why it's hard to catch yourself
- The implication if it ships uncaught
- Court Reportcard caught it

**LinkedIn version:**
> "One of the most common transcript errors: 'bear' vs. 'bare.' Easy to type. Easy to miss when you've been in a transcript for three hours. Hard to explain to a client.
>
> Court Reportcard flags homophone substitutions before the job goes out. Small catch. Big difference."

**X/Twitter version:**
> "bear / bare. One letter. One very awkward client call.
> Court Reportcard catches it before it leaves your desk."

**Instagram caption:**
> "Homophones are the ones that get you.
> You read what you meant to write — not what's actually there.
> That's what Court Reportcard is for. [screenshot of catch]"

## 2. Reporter Story / Testimonial

**Purpose:** Social proof from a peer. More credible than any feature claim.

**Structure:**
- Their situation (relatable, specific)
- What changed
- Quote or paraphrase (with permission)

**Facebook version:**
> "[Name], a [X]-year reporter from [state/region], told us she used to spend the last 20 minutes of every transcript doing a final read-through — 'just in case.'
>
> Now she runs Court Reportcard and uses that 20 minutes for the next job.
>
> That's the whole point."

If sharing without attribution, generalize:
> "We heard from a reporter last week who said this: [quote]. That's exactly what we built it for."

## 3. Transparency / Privacy Post

**Purpose:** Pre-empt the privacy concern. Build trust proactively.

**Structure:**
- Acknowledge the concern is valid
- Explain what happens with data, plainly
- Invite questions

**LinkedIn version:**
> "We know the first question a lot of court reporters have is: what happens to my transcript when I upload it?
>
> It's the right question. These are legal documents. Here's the honest answer: [plain-language data explanation].
>
> If you have more specific questions — especially if you work in sensitive areas like criminal cases or family court — reach out. We'll answer directly."

## 4. Education / Quick Tip

**Purpose:** Provide value without asking for anything. Build credibility over time.

**Structure:**
- One useful fact or tip related to transcript accuracy
- Why it matters
- Optional soft connection to the product

**Instagram / TikTok:**
> "The most-missed word in transcripts? 'Not.'
> As in: 'The defendant did [not] recall.' One word. Completely changes the record.
> Worth a specific check every time."

**X/Twitter:**
> "Common transcript miss: 'did' vs. 'didn't.' Easy to drop the contraction mid-flow. Worth a dedicated pass before you submit."

## 5. Feature Announcement

**Purpose:** Announce a new feature without sounding like a changelog.

**Structure:**
- What the feature does for the reporter (not what it is technically)
- Why it matters to them specifically
- Call to action (try it, learn more)

**LinkedIn version:**
> "We added [feature name] today.
>
> What that means for you: [plain-language benefit — e.g., 'you can now check flagged errors in context without leaving the transcript view'].
>
> If you've been waiting on this one, it's ready. [link]"

---

## 6. Industry Acknowledgment (When News Happens)

**Purpose:** Show you're paying attention to the profession. Use sparingly and only when genuine.

**Structure:**
- Acknowledge the event / issue
- One sentence on why it matters
- No sales pitch

**Facebook version (e.g., NCRA Conference):**
> "NCRA 2026 starts this week. For everyone heading to [city] — safe travels. It's one of the best places in the profession to learn and connect.
> (If you're there and want to talk, we'll be around.)"

**When the news is hard (layoffs, controversy, strike):**
> Pause promotional content. If you say anything, acknowledge the situation with a single human sentence. Then go quiet until it passes. Do not pivot to product.

---

## 7. Myth-Busting

**Purpose:** Address common misconceptions directly. Builds trust with skeptics.

**Structure:**
- State the myth plainly
- Correct it simply and without condescension
- Optional: explain why the myth exists (shows empathy)

**TikTok / Instagram:**
> "Common question we get: 'Does Court Reportcard store my transcripts?'
> Short answer: no. [explanation of actual data model]
> The concern is completely valid — legal transcripts contain sensitive information. That's exactly why we built it this way."

**LinkedIn:**
> "We've seen some confusion about what Court Reportcard actually is — specifically whether it's a transcription tool or an AI that could replace a reporter.
>
> It's neither. It's a proofreading assistant. It reviews what you've already transcribed and flags errors before the job leaves your desk. The reporter is still the professional doing the work.
>
> We'll keep saying this as many times as it takes."
