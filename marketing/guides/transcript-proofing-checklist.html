The Transcript Proofing Checklist

Eight quick passes every certified court reporter can run before turning in a transcript — the same categories of errors Court Reportcard checks for automatically.

A transcript rarely fails because of one big mistake — it fails, gets flagged, or quietly erodes a reporter's reputation because of small ones that slip past a tired final read. Run through this before you certify, or use it to train a new reporter on what "proofread" actually means.

1

Names & Proper Nouns

  • Every name spelled consistently across the caption, appearances page, and body.
  • Spellings confirmed on the record where a witness or attorney spelled a name aloud.
  • Company, product, and place names verified, especially ones that sound like ordinary words.
2

Speaker ID & Colloquy

  • Speaker tags match who is actually speaking during fast cross-talk.
  • Q&A attribution is correct after objections, sidebars, or a change of examining attorney.
  • Overlapping speech is broken into separate lines per speaker, not merged.
3

Punctuation That Changes Meaning

  • Commas around restrictive clauses — a misplaced comma can change the claim entirely.
  • Question marks vs. periods on lines that read either way, especially in rapid cross.
  • Em dashes for self-interruption used consistently, not swapped with ellipses.
4

Numbers, Dates & Figures

  • Dollar amounts and quantities double-checked — a transposed digit in damages is serious.
  • Dates consistent with day of week and other dates referenced earlier.
  • Exhibit and page numbers match the numbering used elsewhere in the case file.
5

Homophones & Sound-Alikes

  • Classic pairs: there/their, to/too/two, your/you're, principal/principle, affect/effect.
  • Legal sound-alikes: cite/site/sight, council/counsel, deposition/disposition.
  • Technical/industry terms spelled correctly even when they sound like ordinary words.
6

Formatting & Certification

  • Certificate page present, signed, and matching your state's required language.
  • Appearances page complete — every attorney, party, and role accounted for.
  • Line numbering, margins, font consistent with your jurisdiction's rules.
7

Recess & Procedural Notations

  • Every recess/off-the-record break is noted with a timestamp where required.
  • Resumption of proceedings is clearly marked, including changes in who's present.
  • Objections and rulings notated in the format your court or firm expects.
8

Final Read-Through Pass

  • Read once for sense — does each answer logically follow the question asked?
  • Flag anything that made you pause on first read; it's usually worth resolving.
  • Step away and re-read after a break — fatigue causes most missed errors.
